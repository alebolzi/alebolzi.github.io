BTC Multi-Factor Model

Quantitative Multi-Factor Framework for Bitcoin Macro-Cycle Analysis and Market Regime Classification

Abstract

This document presents the BTC Multi-Factor Model, a quantitative framework designed to evaluate Bitcoin's macro-cycle state through four independent market dimensions: Halving Cycle (structural timing), Fear & Greed Index (sentiment), MA200 Deviation (trend valuation), and Monthly RSI (momentum). Each indicator is normalized to a unified [0,1] scale—where 0 represents accumulation/undervaluation and 1 represents distribution/euphoria—and aggregated into a composite score via arithmetic mean. The output provides a probabilistic market positioning signal intended for macro-level strategic decision support.

1.Introduction

Bitcoin markets exhibit recurring cyclical patterns driven by supply dynamics (halvings), participant psychology (fear/greed cycles), trend deviations from long-term means, and momentum oscillations. Individual indicators capture partial information; their synthesis enables more robust regime classification.

The model addresses three core objectives:

  1. Multi-dimensional synthesis: Combine structural, sentiment, valuation, and momentum signals into a unified metric.
  2. Normalization: Map heterogeneous inputs (days, index values, percentages, oscillator readings) to a common probabilistic scale.
  3. Actionable interpretation: Translate composite scores into discrete market regimes with historical precedent.

Scope & Limitations

This framework operates at the macro-cycle level (monthly+ timeframes). It is not designed for short-term trading signals, price targets, or timing precision. The model is experimental and intended for analytical/educational purposes only.

Equal weighting is applied across all four factors. The resulting score S ∈ [0, 1] represents the model's aggregate assessment of market conditions, where lower values indicate historically favorable accumulation zones and higher values indicate euphoric/distribution regimes.

Fundamental Premise

📅 Temporal Model (Not Price-Based)

This component operates exclusively on calendar time, not price action. Historical analysis of Bitcoin's four completed halving cycles reveals a consistent temporal pattern: approximately ⅔ of cycle duration exhibits bullish price behavior, while ⅓ exhibits bearish/corrective behavior. The model maps these temporal zones relative to the halving date as anchor point (t=0).

Cycle Length Calculation

The cycle length (CYCLE_LENGTH) is computed dynamically as:

Dynamic Cycle Length Formula CYCLE_LENGTH = (Halving[n] − Halving[n−1] + Halving[n−1] − Halving[n−2]) / 2
  • Takes the average of the two most recent halving intervals to account for minor variations in block time
  • Typical range: ~1,400 – 1,450 days (~3.85 years)
  • Recalibrates automatically after each new halving event

Temporal Phase Mapping

Given elapsed days since halving (t_elapsed), the normalized position within cycle is:

Cycle Progress p = telapsed / CYCLE_LENGTH    (where p ∈ [0, 1])

The model classifies time into three distinct zones based on historical price behavior:

Cycle Position (p) Elapsed Days* Temporal Zone Historical Behavior & Score Output
0.00 – 0.33 0 – ~470 BULL ZONE Post-halving supply shock; early bullish momentum. Score rises toward 1.0 as this phase progresses (approaching euphoria/top formation by ~33%).
0.33 – 0.67 ~470 – ~940 BEAR ZONE Mid-cycle corrective phase. Score decreases from 1.0 → 0.0 across this window. Maximum accumulation opportunity (score ≈ 0.0) occurs near the 66% mark (~940–1000 days).
0.67 – 1.00 ~940 – 1,420 BULL ZONE Pre-next-halving rally; late-cycle recovery. Score rises from 0.0 toward higher values as next halving approaches (potential renewed euphoria).

*Approximate values based on CYCLE_LENGTH = 1,420 days. Actual values scale dynamically.

Non-Linear Score Curve

Crucially, the mapping from cycle position p to score S_halving is not linear. The score follows an inverted curve within the bear zone:

Key Score Anchors (Empirical)
Cycle Position Days Post-Halving Shalving Score Interpretation
p = 0.00 Day 0 (Halving) ~0.50 Neutral starting point
p ≈ 0.33 ~470 days → 1.00 Peak Euphoria (post-first-bull top)
p = 0.50 ~710 days ~0.50 Mid-correction transition
p ≈ 0.67 ~950–1000 days → 0.00 Maximum Opportunity (cycle bottom)
p = 1.00 ~1,420 days ~0.70–0.90 Pre-halving rally (elevated again)

Key Characteristics

  1. Date-driven logic: The model does not analyze current price, trend, or momentum—it purely evaluates where we are in calendar time relative to the halving anchor.
  2. Inverted curve in bear zone: Unlike naive linear mapping, the score peaks at ~33% (end of first bull) and bottoms at ~66% (mid-cycle trough). This reflects the empirical observation that the worst time to enter is ~500 days post-halving (euphoric top), while the best time to accumulate is ~1000 days post-halving (capitulation bottom).
  3. Asymmetric bull/bear split: Reflects empirical observation that Bitcoin spends ~67% of cycle time in bullish conditions and ~33% in bearish/corrective phases—but the score intensity varies non-linearly within each zone.
  4. Self-similar structure: Each halving cycle exhibits this temporal pattern with high consistency across Bitcoin's history (2012, 2016, 2020 halvings).

4.3 MA200 Deviation Analysis

Calculation Pipeline

  1. Retrieve last 200 BTC/USDT daily closing prices from Binance
  2. Compute Simple Moving Average: MA200 = Σ(close[i]) / 200
  3. Calculate percentage deviation: dev = ((close[0] - MA200) / MA200) × 100
  4. Apply clamping normalization (see §4.3.2 below)

Normalization Logic: Clamped Linear Mapping

⚙️ The ±30% Bound (Cap Mechanism)

The raw deviation value (dev) can theoretically range from approximately -90% (extreme bear markets, e.g., 2018 bottom) to +300%+ (parabolic bull runs, e.g., 2021 peak). To prevent extreme outliers from dominating the composite score, the model applies a hard clamp (cap) at ±30%. Values beyond this boundary are truncated to the boundary value (saturation).

Mathematical Formulation

Clamped Normalization Function Sma200 = clamp( (dev + 30) / 60 , 0 , 1 )

Where the clamp(x, min, max) function enforces:

Piecewise Definition 
if dev ≤ -30:   S_ma200 = 0          ← Hard floor (saturated minimum)
if dev ≥ +30:   S_ma200 = 1          ← Hard ceiling (saturated maximum)
else:           S_ma200 = (dev + 30) / 60   ← Linear interpolation

Why ±30%? Rationale for Boundary Selection

The ±30% cap was selected based on empirical analysis of Bitcoin's historical deviation distribution across multiple market cycles (2013–2024):

  1. Statistical coverage: Approximately 95%+ of trading days fall within the [-30%, +30%] range under normal market conditions. Only extreme black-swan events or parabolic blow-offs exceed these bounds.
  2. Mean-reversion anchor points: Historical data shows that deviations beyond ±30% have high mean-reversion probability within 3–6 months. The 2018 bottom (-45%) reverted to mean within 180 days; the 2021 top (+70%) reverted within 90 days.
  3. Signal saturation prevention: Without capping, a +70% deviation would produce a normalized score of (70+30)/60 = 1.67, which exceeds the [0,1] target range and would disproportionately dominate the composite score (overweighting this factor 67% above its equal-share allocation).
  4. Asymmetric risk acknowledgment: While the bounds appear symmetric (±30%), Bitcoin's actual distribution is positively skewed — extreme positive deviations (+50% to +100%) occur more frequently than extreme negative deviations (-40% to -60%). The symmetric cap intentionally compresses the upside tail more aggressively than downside tail, reflecting the premise that euphoria is more dangerous than panic for long-term accumulation strategies.

🔧 Future Calibration Note: The ±30% boundary is a tunable hyperparameter. In future iterations, this cap may be reduced (e.g., to ±25% or ±20%) to increase sensitivity to moderate extensions, or asymmetrically adjusted (e.g., -35%/+25%) to reflect different risk tolerances on the downside vs. upside. Any recalibration should be backtested against full-cycle historical data to validate regime classification accuracy.

Timeframe
Monthly candles (1M)
Period

RSI Computation

Following J. Welles Wilder's original formulation:

Algorithm Steps
  1. Calculate monthly price changes: Δ = close[t] - close[t-1]
  2. Separate gains (Δ > 0) and losses (Δ < 0); set losses to absolute values
  3. Compute smoothed averages: AvgGain, AvgLoss (Wilder's exponential smoothing)
  4. Relative Strength: RS = AvgGain / AvgLoss
  5. RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

Threshold Interpretation

RSI Range Technical State Normalized Signal
< 30 Oversold (extreme weakness) 0.0 – 0.3 Accumulate
30 – 50 Bearish momentum 0.3 – 0.5 Neutral-Bearish
50 – 70 Bullish momentum 0.5 – 0.7 Neutral-Bullish
> 70 Overbought (exhaustion risk) 0.7 – 1.0 Distribute

Note: On monthly timeframes, extreme RSI readings (<20 or >80) are statistically rare but highly significant, often coinciding with major cycle pivots. The monthly RSI acts as a low-pass filter that confirms macro-trend structure beyond short-term noise.

5.Context-Aware Reasoning Engine

5.1 The Blind Spot of Raw Numerical Scores

⚠️ Critical Limitation Identified

During extensive backtesting and live observation, a fundamental limitation emerged: the composite numerical score alone is insufficient for robust decision-making. A high individual factor score in a bear-phase context may indicate a sell signal rather than euphoria; conversely, a low score during a bull phase may flag a top formation rather than an accumulation opportunity. The raw number lacks situational awareness.

The Core Problem: Context-Independent Interpretation

Consider two scenarios that yield similar composite scores but demand diametrically opposed actions:

Both scenarios cluster around the 0.45–0.55 neutral band, yet one represents a dangerous distribution trap while the other is a high-conviction entry point. The composite score alone cannot distinguish these cases—it requires conditional logic that evaluates factor interactions within temporal context.

5.2 Solution: Context-Aware Scenario Classification

🧠 Reasoning Engine Architecture

To address this blind spot, the model implements a secondary inference layer—the Reasoning Engine—that applies rule-based conditional logic atop the raw factor outputs. Rather than interpreting scores in isolation, the engine evaluates combinations of factors against temporal regime context (bull vs. bear phase determined by Halving Cycle position).

Temporal Regime Determination

The engine first establishes the temporal context using the Halving Cycle component:

Regime Classification Rules 
BEAR_PHASE:  days ∈ [500, 1000] post-halving
BULL_PHASE:  days ∉ [500, 1000] post-halving (all other times)

This binary classification splits the cycle into its empirically observed bearish (mid-cycle correction) and bullish (early/late cycle) windows, providing the context frame for all subsequent conditional evaluations.

5.4 Decision Hierarchy & Precedence

When multiple scenario conditions are simultaneously satisfied (overlap cases), the engine applies a precedence hierarchy based on risk severity:

Priority Scenario Trigger Conditions Rationale
1 (Highest) BULL TRAP Bear phase + Overvaluation + Euphoria Highest risk of catastrophic loss (selling into bear trap rally before -60%+ drawdown). Takes precedence over all other signals when active.
2 CYCLE TOP Late-bull phase + Extension + Confirmation Second-highest urgency. Missing cycle tops results in holding through -80% drawdowns. Profit-taking imperative overrides accumulation signals.
3 CYCLE BOTTOM Late-bear phase + Deep Undervaluation High-conviction entry but lower immediate urgency than exit signals. Missing bottom delays entry but doesn't destroy capital.
4 (Lowest) BUY THE DIP Bull phase + Pullback + Fear Lowest priority. Dip-buying opportunities recur frequently in bull markets; missing one dip is recoverable. Never triggers if higher-priority sell signals are active.

5.5 Implementation Notes

⚙️ Engineering Considerations: The Reasoning Engine operates as a secondary inference layer that consumes the same raw factor outputs used for composite score calculation. It does not replace the numerical scoring system but augments it with contextual intelligence. In production implementations, the engine outputs both: (1) the raw composite score for quantitative tracking, and (2) the scenario classification string ("BULL TRAP", "BUY THE DIP", etc.) for qualitative decision support. Users are advised to weigh both outputs—using the score for trend monitoring and the scenario label for actionable trade decisions.

5.6 Limitations of the Reasoning Approach

  1. Rule rigidity: Current implementation uses hard-coded thresholds (e.g., days ≥ 500, MA_norm > 0.55). These boundaries are heuristic approximations derived from limited historical samples (4 complete cycles). Future iterations may incorporate fuzzy logic or machine learning classifiers to handle boundary ambiguity.
  2. Binary regime oversimplification: The bull/bear phase classification reduces complex multi-phase cycle dynamics to a binary switch. Real cycles exhibit transitional zones (early-bull → mid-bull → late-bull → early-bear → mid-bear → late-bear) that the current engine compresses into two states.
  3. No probability output: Unlike the composite score (which provides continuous granularity), scenario classification is deterministic and categorical. The engine does not output "70% probability of BULL TRAP"—it either triggers or doesn't. Confidence estimation requires additional statistical modeling.
  4. Single-asset calibration: All thresholds are calibrated specifically to Bitcoin's volatility profile and cycle cadence. Applying identical rules to Ethereum (shorter cycles, different halving dynamics) or altcoins would require independent parameter optimization.

7.Limitations & Risk Disclosure

7.1 Model Limitations

Limitation Category Description & Implication
Lagging Indicators MA200 and RSI are inherently backward-looking; they confirm trends rather than anticipate reversals. Signal latency may range from days (RSI) to weeks (MA200).
Cap Sensitivity The ±30% MA200 boundary is a heuristic choice. If future cycles exhibit systematically larger/smaller deviations (due to institutional adoption reducing volatility, or leverage amplifying it), the cap may require recalibration to maintain discriminative power.
Score Ambiguity As detailed in §5.1, the composite numerical score alone cannot distinguish contextually opposite scenarios (bull trap vs. buy-the-dip). The Reasoning Engine partially addresses this but introduces its own limitations (rule rigidity, binary regime classification).
Regime Dependence Historical normalization parameters assume stable market microstructure. Black swan events, regulatory shocks, or technological disruptions may invalidate baseline assumptions.
Equal Weighting The arithmetic mean assumes equal factor importance. In practice, factor relevance varies by cycle phase (e.g., sentiment dominates mania phases; valuation dominates bottoms).
No Causality The model identifies correlations, not causal mechanisms. High scores do not guarantee imminent crashes; low scores do not guarantee immediate recoveries.
Single-Asset Scope Calibrated exclusively for Bitcoin. Application to altcoins or broader crypto indices requires independent validation and parameter recalibration.
